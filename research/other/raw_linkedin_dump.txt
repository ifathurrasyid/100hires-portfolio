Last quarter a client asked me which intent signal they should use. Hiring data? Funding? Website visits?

I told them to use all of them, but not how they expected.

Most people pick one signal, build a campaign around it, and pray. That's backwards. You don't know which signal predicts a response until you let the data tell you.

Here's the framework we use across 50+ clients.

Run 3 campaigns at once:

1. Entire TAM. No signals. Just firmographics. This is your baseline — and sometimes it wins.

2. Focused segments. One signal per campaign. Hiring SDRs. Recently funded. Using a competitor tool.

3. Trigger-based. Real-time events. Job posting today. Funding this week. Website visit yesterday.

Now here's the move nobody talks about.

Every positive response across all 3, take that company and run it through every signal source you have. Pull hiring data, tech stack, funding history, news, social activity. Everything.

Look for patterns.

Maybe the companies that respond all hired a VP of Sales in the last 90 days. Maybe they all use HubSpot. Maybe they all posted about a specific pain point on LinkedIn.

You wouldn't have found that by guessing upfront.

We also run a catchall workflow in Clay with Clayagent, every signal we can think of, just "what's notable about this company right now?" Sometimes the signal that predicts responses is something you'd never think to look for.

Sometimes you genuinely don't know WHY someone responded. That's fine. Not every response has a readable signal. But the ones that do give you your next 10 campaigns.

Stop picking signals from a menu. Let the data tell you what works.

-This might not be the case for you, but It typically costs about $40k per head to hire an engineer and it's common for most heads of engineering to be behind on hiring

Words like "might", "typically", "it's common" soften the email and get's more replies.

There are 9 variables I test in a cold email. 

They're not equally important, and the order you test them in matters.

Here's the hierarchy, from most impactful to least (in my opinion... of course):

(1) The offer and CTA. 

This is the biggest lever by a wide margin. Most "meeting ask" CTAs cap out at 2 to 3% reply rates. Offer-based CTAs, a real audit, industry benchmarks, a short video, something concrete, consistently outperform them.

(2) The hook. 

Your first line decides whether they keep reading. It needs to make the reason for reaching out feel believable. Not flattery, not fake personalization, not a marketing-style setup. A real observation, a pointed question, or relevant social proof.


(3) The problem statement. 

This should come directly from your sales call recordings, not your website. Your website is one-to-many. Sales calls are one-to-one. If your problem line sounds like something any competitor could write, it's not specific enough.

(4) The value prop. 

Your value prop has two jobs: show you understand what they want, then explain how you deliver it in a way that's actually believable. If it sounds like a generic claim any competitor could make, rewrite it.

Those are the top 4. 

The rest are: Social proof, objection handling, personalization, tone, and subject line. 

These matter, but only after the first four are working. 

Don't layer in personalization to save an email that hasn't converted on the fundamentals yet.
